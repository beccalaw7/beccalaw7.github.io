The Jet Stream

Our weather patterns here in the UK are largely affected by the North Atlantic jet stream which flows from America in the west to Europe in the east. It is temperature differences in the atmosphere that lead to pressure differences which combine with the Earth’s rotation to force air to move around; we call this wind. Jet streams can be found all over the world at around 9-16km above the Earth’s surface which is just below the tropopause, the boundary between the troposphere and the stratosphere.

Jet streams can be thousands of miles long and hundreds of miles wide, travelling at fast speeds of up to 200mph! The jet stream will shift up and down depending on the season and become strongest during the winter because the contrast between the cold polar air mass and the warm tropical air mass is greatest.

We tend to see weather fronts developing at the boundaries between these air masses and you may hear them being called ‘frontal systems’. A low pressure stream forces the warm air upwards through the centre of a spiralling stream of cold air; it is moist so condenses to form clouds when it reaches its dew point.

If the jet stream is stationary it brings regular depressions to the same area, but if the warm air moves further north than it usually does, or the cold air moves further south than it usually does, the jet stream buckles and causes the depressions to move to different areas. This can also block the movement of depressions.
