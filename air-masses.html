Air Masses

Air masses are grouped depending on their typical representative temperature and humidity. There are six main air masses that affect the UK climate and they get named depending on where they come from. Air masses can be continental; originating over land, or maritime; originating over sea, and Arctic, Antarctic, equatorial, tropical or polar depending on where they have come from.

Tropical Continental

This air mass comes from North Africa and the Sahara so it brings with it high temperatures and sometimes a bit of sand from the Saharan dust storms. This usually leaves an orange film on our cars after rain showers. It is most common to influence us during the summer months (June, July and August).

Tropical Maritime

From the name you can tell this air mass travels over the sea; it comes from the Atlantic between the Azores and Bermuda. During this air mass we experience south-westerly winds and low cloud bringing drizzle. This is because the air mass is warm and moist so it becomes quickly unstable when travelling over the cold sea. However, it is a warmer air stream so it does tend to raise temperatures above average.

Polar Continental

This air mass comes from Eastern Europe and Russia and brings cooler temperatures that may go below zero. It is experienced only from November to April, but if it does come over during the summer months where the land is warmer the air mass would be called tropical continental. When it travels a long way across the North Sea we experience rain or snow showers because the air mass picks up moisture over the sea. However, if the air mass travels a shorter sea path over the English Channel then the weather is likely to be clear skies with some morning frosts.

Polar Maritime

A north-westerly air stream brings this air mass over from northern Canada and Greenland and it usually affects the British Isles. It will get gradually warmer as it travels across the North Atlantic and will become more unstable, picking up moisture, which contrasts its initial dry state and brings frequent showers. During the summer when the land is warmer than the sea the heaviest showers occur in the East. But during the winter hail and thunder are experienced in the north and west of the British Isles because convection is most vigorous over the warm sea.

Arctic Maritime

This air mass is a colder, drier version of the polar maritime air mass because it has a shorter distance across the sea to make. It is unusual to receive arctic air during the summer but when we do it brings with it thunderstorms, heavy showers and cooler temperatures. The cold temperatures are understandable when you know that the air mass originates over the North Pole and Arctic Ocean. Sometimes we get widespread, heavy snowfall if there is a low pressure system working its way into this polar air mass but often the sky remains clear. This air mass does not generally bring as cold conditions as the polar continental air mass for much of Britain, but for northern Scotland this is the coldest air mass.

Returning Polar Maritime

This is another version of the polar maritime air mass but it has a longer distance to travel across the sea coming over the North Atlantic. When the air mass journeys southwards it becomes unstable, picking up moisture, but when it begins to move north-east it becomes more stable as it travels over cooler water. It only brings large amounts of stratocumulus clouds with it so mostly dry weather.
