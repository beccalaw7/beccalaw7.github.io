How do Clouds Form?

The atmosphere is composed of gasses; 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen and 1% argon and ‘other gasses’. The ‘other gasses’ includes a very important invisible gas called water vapour. The percentage of water vapour in the atmosphere is dependent on the temperature because it enters the atmosphere through the evaporation of water from the Earth's surface, both land and sea. The higher the temperature, the higher the rate of evaporation and so the more water vapour is in the atmosphere. At 30°C air can contain up to 4% water vapour, whereas at -40°C it can only hold around 0.2%.

Clouds form when invisible water vapour cools, collides and condenses onto cloud condensation nuclei. These are particles of dust and salt which are known as aerosols. The now visible water droplets continue to stick to the cloud condensation nuclei and clump together forming clouds. To give you an idea of size, water droplets are a whole order of magnitude 6 larger than water vapour so if water vapour was a sugar cube then 1 water droplet would be the size of a campervan!

The height at which a cloud forms is the deciding factor as to whether the water droplets will be liquid or ice. Water freezes at 0°C right? Wrong, when it comes to the atmosphere and clouds, water droplets below 0°C are only ‘supercooled’ and do not freeze until -30°C. However, if a supercooled water droplet comes into contact with any surface it will freeze immediately.

The point at which clouds begin to condense and form is called the condensation level or dew point; when the air is saturated with water vapour and cannot hold anymore. This can happen in two ways: the amount of water vapour in the air increases, usually through evaporation, so it cannot hold any more water vapour. The air cools to its dew point, the point where condensation occurs, so the air becomes unstable to hold any more water vapour.

We don't see as many clouds on warm sunny days because warmer air can hold more water vapour, but often large cumulus clouds (the fluffy cauliflower ones) form in the afternoon. This is because as the warm air rises, its temperature decreases and so its ability to hold lots of water vapour decreases, therefore condensation occurs, forming clouds.


There are five main factors that cause clouds to form:

1. Surface Heating: The ground is heated by the sun which causes the air in contact with it to warm up and rise in columns called thermals. These usually produce Cumulus clouds.

2. Topography or Orographic Forcing: These are scientific words for the shape of the land. For instance if air is forced to rise over a mountain barrier it cools as it rises and condenses, forming clouds. This usually forms layered, lenticular clouds.

3. Frontal: A front is a boundary between two air masses; a warm moist air mass and a cool dry air mass. When the warmer air mass rises over a mass of cold air the warm air cools and reaches its dew point so clouds form.

4. Convergence: When streams of air meet and flow together they are forced over each other (the cooler denser one will sink underneath) which forms clouds. Often Cumulus and showery conditions follow this.

5. Turbulence: A sudden change in the wind speed with height can create turbulent eddies in the air which cause air to rise and cool, forming clouds.
