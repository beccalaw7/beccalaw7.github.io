Asperitas Clouds

This cloud is extra special because it is a new cloud type that was proposed by the Cloud Appreciation Society’s founder Gavin Pretor-Pinney in 2008 due to the sheer amount of unknown clouds he had been receiving from members that appeared to look similar. In June 2015 ‘Asperitas’ was officially accepted into the World Meteorological Organisation’s International Cloud Atlas; the reference system used by meteorologists around the world. The new Aspiratus cloud is the first cloud formation to be added to the WMO cloud atlas since ‘Cirrus Intorus’ in 1951.

It used to be known as the ‘Undulatus Asperatus’ cloud which comprises of the word ‘undulatus’ which has been around for a while and means to curl, or undulate. The name ‘Asperitas’ means roughened or agitated which is accurate if you have ever seen one because they tend to look like the underside of a rough sea.

It is unknown as to how they form but they are often seen following convective thunderstorms in North America but never actually turn into storms themselves. Some say the clouds form in similar conditions to the Mammatus clouds, very unstable, and even that they are formed when Mammatus clouds descend into areas of the sky where the wind direction changes with height causing the rippling waves. Mammatus clouds are udder-like pouches forming at the base of a Cumulonimbus (storm) cloud, often underneath the anvil. They are caused by powerful downdraughts when pockets of cold, moist air sink rapidly from the upper parts of the cloud.
