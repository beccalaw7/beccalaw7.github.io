An Introduction to Cloud Classification

When you look into the sky you may see a range of different cloud types and it can sometimes look a bit of a muddle. However, it is possible to organise them into different categories and name them based on their characteristics and the system we use now is all thanks to a man named Luke Howard.

Luke Howard was born on the 28th November 1772 in London. He never trained as a Meteorologist or scientist; he was actually a Pharmacist by trade. He had an enthusiasm for the weather and for 30 years he recorded measurements using his thermometer and barometer as well as visual observations.

Originally clouds were thought to be too short-lived and changeable to be classified. Sailors and farmers just described them by colour and form; popular ones included white, dark, woolly, buttermilk and mackerel skies. Jean Baptiste Lamarck was a French scientist who made his own cloud classification system, but he was too vague and his names were in French and not accepted on a worldwide scale. The international language of science is Latin; therefore Luke Howard set out to make his own set of classifications.

Howard was influenced by John Dalton's ‘Meteorological Observations’ 1793 who said that cloud droplets do not float, they continually fall under the influence of gravity. From this, Howard concluded in 1854 that clouds were not “airy nothings” but subject to “the same fixed laws which pervade every other department of nature”.

    In December 1802 Luke Howard, aged 30, presented his paper ‘On the Modifications of Clouds’ to the Askesian society. He split clouds into three main categories:
  1. Cirrus, which he described as “parallel, flexuous or diverging fibres, extensible in any or all directions”.
  2. Cumulus, which he described as “convex or conical heaps, increasing upwards from a horizontal base”.
  3. Stratus, which he described as “a widely extended, continuous, horizontal sheet, increasing from below”.

    He then combined the initial three cloud names to form four new cloud types:
  1. Cirro-cumulus, which he described as “small, well defined, roundish masses in close horizontal arrangement”.
  2. Cirro-stratus, which he described as “horizontal or slightly inclined masses, attenuated towards a part or the whole of their circumference, bent downward, or undulated, separate, or in groups consisting of small clouds having these characters”.
  3. Cumulostratus, which he described as “the cirrostratus blended with the cumulus, either appearing intermixed with heaps of the latter, or super-adding a widespread structure to its base”.
  4. Cumulo-cirrus-stratus/Cumulonimbus, which he described as “a cloud, or system of clouds, from which rain is falling”.

    He also published:
  • The Climate of London (first edition 1818, second edition 1830)
  • Seven lectures on meteorology (1837)
  • A cycle of eighteen years in the seasons of Britain (1842)
  • Barometrographia (1847)
  • He was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society on 8 March 1821 and joined the British (now Royal) Meteorological Society on 7 May 1850, only a month after the society was founded

He died in London on 21 March 1864 aged 91.

    Quick guide to the Latin prefixes and suffixes:

  • Stratus/strato = blanket-like, flat, smooth
  • Cumulus/cumulo = heap or stack, like cauliflowers
  • Cirrus/cirro = wisp or curl
  • Alto = Medium level
  • Nimbus/Nimbo = Rain-bearing cloud
