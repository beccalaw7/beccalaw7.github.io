Contrails

Contrails is short for ‘condensation trails’ and form behind an aircraft’s exhaust because of the invisible water vapour in the air condensing onto aerosols being emitted from the plane. Some of the products of an aircraft exhaust are involved in contrail formation, and the rest contributes to air pollution. Some of the common gas and solid products include: carbon dioxide (CO2), nitrogen oxides (NOx), carbon monoxide (CO), hydrocarbons such as methane (CH4), sulfates (SOx), soot and metal particles. Aircraft fly at high altitudes where temperatures are very low so the droplets freeze, possibly even going straight from the gas state to solid state (sublimation).

There are three main types of contrails and these are short-lived, persistent (non-spreading) and persistent (spreading) trails. Short-lived trails look like tiny white lines following a plane but disappearing shortly after it has moved on because there is a lack of water vapour in the air and any ice crystals that are able to form quickly transition back into the vapour state. Persistent non-spreading trails remain in the sky once a plane has flown over but are only about as thin as your finger when held up to the sky. Persistent spreading contrails are the result of a rich supply of water vapour and can cover larger areas of the sky than the other two varieties. Some will thicken out into cirrostratus or cirrocumulus clouds depending on the conditions in the Troposphere.
